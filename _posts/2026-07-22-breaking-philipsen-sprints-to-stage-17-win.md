It has been recently reported that Philipsen could now win the second green jersey of his career

Belgium's Jasper Philipsen won stage 17 of the Tour de France following a bunch sprint.

The Alpecin-Premier Tech rider powered over the line with a good gap over his rivals to win his first stage in what had been a frustrating three-week race.

The 28-year-old won the prestigious green points jersey in 2023, but had struggled in this race to catch compatriot Tim Merlier in the final phases of sprints.

After stage seven, he was seen on social media tossing his bike in frustration after his illustrious team-mate, the powerhouse classics legend Mathieu van der Poel had mis-timed his lead-out and Philipsen became blocked in.

But after a hilly start on a warm day on the 174.7km stage from Chambery to Voiron near the Alps, Philipsen enjoyed a powerful lead-out from Van der Poel and was able to sustain his power all the way to the line.

The stage, which flattened in the latter stages to suit the sprinters, could have worked out differently as several riders attempted to break away from the peloton. 

More than 40 riders eventually got away, including current green jersey wearer Mads Pedersen of Lidl-Trek and British rider Josh Tarling of Netcompany Ineos Cycling plus Lewis Askey of Spain's 2010 World Cup winner Andres Iniesta's NSN Cycling.

Denmark's Pedersen eventually finished eighth, but retained the green jersey by only seven points in the standings over Philipsen in second - setting up an exciting finale in Paris.

It was the sprinters' last chance to battle on the flat until the race's final stage in the French capital on Sunday, which has changed to incorporate a short lapped climb over Montmartre.

"Such a roller-coaster of emotions on this Tour," said a tearful Philipsen after the stage. "The first 12 stages were just horrible - I couldn't find myself; couldn't find the feeling I was hoping for. After 17 stages to take victory, its really one for team for keeping on believing in me.

"I want to thank everybody... I was like an angry bird at the [dinner] table [each night]. Just disappointed and angry, and the team, they had to deal with it. This one is for them for keeping on believing."

It was a quiet day for the riders in the overall battle for the yellow jersey, with leader Tadej Pogacar coming home in the peloton nearly nine minutes behind the bunch sprint. 

Pogacar, 27, of UAE Team Emirates-XRG sits four minutes and 32 seconds ahead of Belgian star Remco Evenepoel of Red Bull-Bora-Hansgrohe, with Pogacar's Mexican team-mate Isaac del Toro a further one minute and 19 seconds in down in third in the overall standings.

Another Briton, Tom Pidcock, came home in the peloton, but one of Pogacar's key domestiques Adam Yates struggled with sickness during the stage after vomiting at the end of Tuesday's time trial, eventually making it over the line more than 26 minutes down. 

The team will assess whether former yellow jersey wearer and one-time Tour stage winner Yates will continue on, with the next three gruelling mountain stages taking place in the Alps - starting with Thursday's stage 18 test to Orcieres-Merlette.

During the stage, cycling's world governing body the UCI issued a statement defending the night-time anti-doping tests carried out by the International Testing Agency on Saturday, which has thrown the spotlight back on doping in the sport.

Questions have been asked about testing carried out in the small hours after two-time winner and main rival to Pogacar, Jonas Vingegaard, who was tested at two o'clock in the morning, crashed out of the race the following day on a simple right-hand corner.

The statement read: "Following a number of reactions in recent days to unannounced anti-doping controls carried out by the International Testing Agency (ITA) during the Tour de France  the Union Cycliste Internationale (UCI) wishes to respond and to reaffirm that the fight against doping is an absolute priority.

"The UCI of course acknowledges that night-time testing can disrupt riders' rest and recovery, and wishes to reiterate that carrying out such controls outside normal hours remains an exceptional measure, reserved for limited and justified circumstances. While fully aware of how onerous such a measure is for riders, the UCI nevertheless underscores that these controls serve an overriding interest: the fight against doping.

"The UCI will continue to support the ITA in carrying out its mission, in full and complete respect for its independence, in the service of a cleaner and more credible sport of cycling."

Sprinters are protected by a line of team domestiques riding in front of them at speed.

Not only does it save energy for the sprinter who does not travel into the wind, it also ensures a good road position compared to their rivals and prevents anyone blocking them just as they are about to launch.

But launch timing in sprinting is everything, and even a lead-out train can get things wrong.

If they delay or go too early, the domestiques can get in the way of their own rider, or a rival can use the train to launch themselves to the line.
