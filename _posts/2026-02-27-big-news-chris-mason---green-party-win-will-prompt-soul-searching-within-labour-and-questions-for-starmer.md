Let's dive into the details: Never before have the Green Party won a Westminster by-election.

So this is a result that will take its place in the history of parliamentary contests between elections.

And it will prompt yet more soul searching within the Labour Party: finishing third was the nightmare result for them.

It makes real the long-standing fear of some in the party that they would face an insurgency not just broadly from the Right, in the shape of Reform UK, but from the Left as well, in the form of the Green Party.

In Gorton and Denton, that is precisely what has happened.

The prime minister will wake up with both a tactical and a strategic question in his mind.

Firstly, should he have allowed the Mayor of Greater Manchester, Andy Burnham, to be Labour's candidate? His supporters in the count centre told me they do think he would have made a significant difference.

And secondly, has Labour's focus on Reform UK left them exposed among some of their regular supporters?

At the 2024 general election, Labour won with a majority of more than 13,000 in Gorton and Denton and secured more than 50% of the vote.

The inquest for some Labour MPs began in the middle of the night.

"Keir needs to own this. He's out of chances," one texted me.

"Everyone knew the only chance to beat both the Greens and Reform was Andy Burnham. You can't claim to be the party of unity when you're adopting the divisive policies of Reform. Vote Labour to stop Reform rings hollow when you're mimicking them."

Labour's deputy leader Lucy Powell made a dash for it immediately after the declaration; reporters, me included, chasing after her shouting questions.

She had, to be fair, done a few interviews before the result was confirmed, but after it looked clear Labour was doomed.

Reform's candidate Matt Goodwin did talk to journalists afterwards, branding the Green Party "mad" and claiming that, unlike Reform, they wouldn't be able to replicate this in many places at a general election.

The Green Party is delighted. I grabbed a quick word with their relatively new leader, Zack Polanski, just moments after the polls closed, and he wore the smile and demeanour of a confident leader.

He can now point to a result that suggests the party's buoyant opinion poll ratings since he became leader have some substance to them. We can expect a victory rally with him and his new MP Hannah Spencer later this morning.

And if you are looking for further evidence that politics is fracturing, unpredictable and wildly competitive, take a look at how the Conservatives fared.

The Tories managed just 706 votes - 1.9% of the votes cast - for a party that was running the country just two years ago.

A final thought that will be part of the conversation in the hours and days after this by election – the suggestion of significant "family voting" as it is known.

The group Democracy Volunteers reported what it called "concerningly high levels of family voting in Gorton and Denton" - which it defines as "where two voters either confer, collude or direct each other on voting".

Manchester City Council insisted "no such issues" were reported by its staff at polling stations.

Labour Party Chair Anna Turley told the BBC the reports were "obviously extremely worrying and concerning" and Reform UK leader Nigel Farage claimed the by-election was "a victory for sectarian voting and cheating".
