Islington seems, on the surface, a family-friendly area. Parents push buggies through quiet leafy side streets of this popular neighbourhood that is also within easy reach of central London.

But this north London borough has one of the lowest fertility rates in the country and mothers are older on average here than in most other parts of the capital.

Islington is a snapshot of a trend also playing out not only in inner London but also other parts of the world.

The situation has been described by the London Assembly as an "existential challenge" for the city as a whole, so what are the reasons behind this and what can be done about it?

Francesca Baker-Brooker, 39, who works in PR, and her husband Andy, 41, an accountant, have decided not to have children.

The couple live in a two-bedroom flat in Islington. They both say they shared a "gut instinct" early in their relationship that parenthood was not for them.

"We like our lifestyle - we like running, going to festivals and gigs," Francesca said.

Soon after that conversation, they moved from a three-bedroom house in Kent to a two-bedroom flat in Islington and Andy had a vasectomy.

"I don't know how feasible it would have been to move to London if we'd wanted to start a family," Andy said.

When people ask if he has a family, Andy says his answer is: "I have a family â my wife."

They say about half of their friends in London do not have children, although they believe that is far less common outside the capital.

"I think it definitely is easier to be child-free in London," he added.

Katie Beck, a policy fellow at the London School of Economics' LSE Cities, said: "People want to have children in London, but there are barriers â namely the cost of living and childcare. 

"People are not able to stay in London to have children, or once they do they're moving to outer boroughs or leaving London altogether."

Katie Beck, a policy fellow at the London School of Economics' LSE Cities, says children are an "indicator species" representing a healthy city

Beck says London's falling rates of children reflects a wider global trend, driven by housing and childcare costs, more women entering higher education and the workforce, changing parenting expectations, fewer teenage pregnancies, and growing concerns about the future. 

She points to boroughs such as Islington, where high poverty, limited green space, expensive housing and dense populations contribute to particularly low child populations.

Students Jess, Charlotte and Milly say it is difficult for their generation to imagine having children in central London

I was keen to find out what the younger generation think, so spoke to a group of students.

Jess Rolf, 23, says having children was "never something she worried about", and trying to build a settled life was "very stressful with the cost of living and the job market".

"I'm from Lincolnshire and people my age back home are buying houses with their partners and talking about starting families.

"I don't think that's on the cards for me."

Charlotte Ward, 26, has said planning ahead is difficult. 

"A lot of people our age are quite disenfranchised.

"You put all your energy into school, university and work, then wonder, 'do I want to bring somebody else into that if I can't even support myself right now?'

"When parents or grandparents ask 'what happens when you have children?', I can say, 'I might not'.

"There's a small element of emancipation from the expectation that you definitely will have children but if I'm progressing in my career and my male counterparts don't have to take time out to have a child, that could put me at a disadvantage."

Millicent Angel, 24, says it is hard to envisage a future when the world feels "so unstable" but she still definitely wants children. 

She adds there's "a million and one things" she would like to do first, including developing her career.

Beck argues that "children are an indicator species". 

"They are a sign of a healthy city. If you see a dearth of children, that rings alarm bells that a city is not functioning as it should," she added.

She describes a child-friendly city as one that is accessible, with active transport and safe affordable housing. 

As the independent think tank Centre for London points out: "London is not becoming a city without families with children, but it is becoming a place where the realities that families with children face are increasingly unequal."

A spokesperson for the Mayor of London, Sir Sadiq Khan said: "The mayor wants London to remain a city for everyone â where people can afford to put down roots, raise a family and build a future.

"That is why tackling London's housing crisis and increasing the supply of genuinely affordable homes is one of his top priorities, alongside measures to support families with the cost of living, including funding free school meals for every child in a state primary school."

They added that he would "continue working with the government to build more affordable homes, support public services and ensure London remains a great place for families and children".

A government spokesperson said: "We've made paternity and unpaid parental leave day one rights through our Employment Rights Act, and have rolled out 30 hours of funded childcare a week to working families." 

The government added that it was also building more council homes to back "families at every stage".
