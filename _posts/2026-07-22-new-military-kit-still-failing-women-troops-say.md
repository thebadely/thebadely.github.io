Here’s what you need to know: Rhian Brown served seven years in the Army Reserve before leaving in 2024 after working as an infantry platoon commander

Women in the armed forces say they are still being issued kit that does not fit properly, five years after a landmark report warned they were at "greater risk of harm" from ill-fitting uniform and equipment.

The 2021 Atherton report said the Ministry of Defence should urgently roll out safer, more appropriate kit for female service personnel by the end of 2022.

Despite improvements, women have been telling the BBC some kit is still poorly fitting, with body armour failing to account for breasts, and shortages of smaller sizes resulting in "competition" among troops.

The Ministry of Defence (MOD) said it ensured everyone had access to the "best equipment they need to protect themselves in combat".

"I never felt that I was neat, tidy, presentable, or in the uniform that I was meant to be in," explains veteran Rhian Brown, who left the service in 2024.

"I never really felt that it protected me or entirely fulfilled the purpose that it was meant to."

Rhian Brown, second from left, says she had difficulty getting clothing or body armour that was small enough for her

Following the report by Dame Sarah Atherton, external, the Ministry of Defence brought out a new version of its latest body armour adapted for female troops in 2022.

It said it offered improved "fit, comfort and mobility while maintaining consistent ballistic protection standards".

Called the Virtus Scalable Tactical Vest, the body armour system has adjustable straps and smaller flat plates.

Brown, 26, who left the Army Reserve after seven years as an infantry platoon commander, wore it in training and on deployments. 

She says the newer version was an improvement, feeling "tight and secure" and looking smarter, "but, ultimately, it still wasn't designed to fit me".

"Women are not flat, we have breasts and curves," Brown continues.

"The plates are flat, it doesn't fit, it is not flexible - fit isn't about vanity, it's about safety."

Brown, from Plymouth, said on one occasion the person issuing body armour told her "you're probably a small... but we don't have any smalls so here, take this medium". 

It comes as the MoD paid for the supply of 2,000 sets of new curved body armour plates for women in the Ukrainian armed forces.

The curved plates allow for breast shape and the firm behind the design claims they will better protect women when compared with the flat plate 

The UK government said it spent Â£4.5bn on military support to Ukraine last year.

It is understood the contract with NP Aerospace, based in Coventry, was in response to a specific operational need and sat outside the UK's wider equipment programmes, which go through their own testing and approval processes.

Seren White, who worked on the design of the new curved armour, said the plates had "been tested with the highest level of rigor".

For industry certification, she explained the plates needed to be tested 36 times - but the new curved plates have been shot almost 300 times.

Seren White was part of the team that designed the new curved plates

Engineers said they were able to debunk some "myths", including that bullets can ricochet off the curve in the ballistic plate.

"Women today are wearing flat plates that have been designed for male torsos," explains Erin Grieve, from NP Aerospace.

"We are trying to address that women are not small men - they're shaped differently."

Grieve said the firm's own research concluded the curved plates provided women with more protection when compared with flat plates.

As it stands, this kit is not available for UK servicewomen.

Erin Grieve says current body armour is designed around the male torso

The BBC spoke to a number of servicewomen who asked to remain anonymous.

One described her body armour as "definitely not female and definitely not good".

Another said there was "none or barely any" female-specific kit available to her and that the body armour did not account for "busty women".

Issues around kit and clothing have persisted.

Ofsted, which inspects MoD training establishment as well as schools, said in October 2025 that for the "fourth consecutive year" women and smaller recruits were being issued with ill-fitting kit and clothing, external.

Its report said: "This renders them at a disadvantage because they often do not appear as well turned out as their peers, or because ill-fitting equipment, such as webbing or rucksacks, prevents them from participating fully in training."

It added "ill-fitting kit poses a very serious risk" in combat situations.

Dr Mary Keeling, from the Centre for Evidence for the Armed Forces, said she was aware a rollout of new female kit and equipment, as well as availability, had been "inconsistent" since Atherton's report. 

She said there had been "good steps in the right direction" but progress had been slow, adding faster changes were needed as the MoD aimed to increase the number of women joining the forces.

Keeling said it was likely improve challenges in recruitment "if women feel welcome by making sure that appropriate kit and equipment are available".

Stacey Denyer was deployed around the world during her 19 years serving in the RAF, including in Iraq and Afghanistan

After the Atherton Inquiry, RAF veteran Stacey Denyer, who left the service in 2023, was one of a number of servicewomen who joined a ministerial roundtable to discuss putting its recommendations into practice.

"In the latter part of my career, they started doing women's fitting boots. It was amazing," she explains. 

"We're not whinging for the sake of whinging - we're making noise because it is a massive safety issue."

Denyer, who is from Taunton in Somerset, added: "It absolutely needs to happen quickly and there is an aspiration for the armed forces to be 30% female by 2030.

"And, if they want to attract women, they need to start looking after them properly, and that starts with kit."

Military clothing sizes have traditionally not matched civilian size guides

Fred Thomas, MP for Plymouth Moor View and a former Royal Marine officer, said it should be "an absolute priority" for the MoD to get body armour that fitted women at a time of heightened global instability.

Thomas was part of a Defence Select Committee hearing that reviewed the Atherton report in April, almost five years on from its publication.

He said he was aware of slow progress around improved kit but the services were "doing their best". 

He added it was "unacceptable for some people to be better protected than others".

The MoD said new ballistic plates would be delivered in 2028 and would "be available in multiple sizes offering a better fit for all service personnel". 

The BBC understands these plates will be flat and not curved.

The MoD added: "We are also developing the next generation of body armour through our Complex Geometry Body Armour Programme, which will enable the procurement of curved body armour plates."

It is understood these could begin to be delivered from 2029.
